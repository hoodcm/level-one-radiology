The spleen is the most commonly injured solid organ in blunt abdominal trauma, so this is the read you will do most often on a trauma service. Whether the patient goes to the OR, to angiography, or to a monitored bed hangs largely on the CT. The lacerations themselves are conspicuous and rarely the problem. The trouble comes from vascular findings read as parenchymal ones, and from grades treated as verdicts.

## Find It, Then Grade It

On the portal venous phase the spleen enhances homogeneously, so lacerations, hematomas, and subcapsular collections read as defects against a uniform background. Perisplenic fluid is often what first pulls your eye to the quadrant.

Grading comes next, and it helps to know what the AAST Organ Injury Scale is really asking: how much of the spleen has lost its blood supply. That is what predicts whether the injury heals or keeps bleeding. The scale was designed around operative findings, so imaging-based grades correlate imperfectly with what the surgeon sees, and the grade alone should not carry the management conversation.

## The Vascular Survey

This is where management gets decided. Active extravasation appears as a focus of contrast that grows and brightens from the arterial to the portal venous phase, because blood is still leaving the circulation while the scanner runs. Say explicitly in the report whether it is contained within the parenchyma or capsule, or free in the peritoneum. Contained bleeding under an intact capsule can be watched in a way free hemorrhage cannot.

A pseudoaneurysm can look similar on a single phase, but it follows blood pool on every phase instead of growing. It is not bleeding at the moment you scan it, and it still matters, because pseudoaneurysms are behind many delayed ruptures.

Then check the neighbors. The blow that injures the spleen also loads the left lower ribs, the left hemidiaphragm, the left kidney, and the pancreatic tail. Walk those four before signing off.

## The 48 to 72 Hour Problem

Delayed splenic rupture is a real entity. A hematoma or pseudoaneurysm that looked stable on arrival can rupture on day two or three, which is why nonoperative management includes follow-up imaging at 48 to 72 hours. If the plan is observation, the initial report should recommend that follow-up explicitly.

## Putting It Together

No single imaging feature dictates management. The team integrates hemodynamic status, injury grade, and the vascular survey, and the decision to take the patient to the OR is entirely the surgeon's, not yours. What your report owes them is each ingredient stated plainly: the grade, any vascular finding by name, and contained versus free. That sequence prevents the two common errors, overcalling a low-grade laceration into an intervention it does not need, and undercalling the pseudoaneurysm that needed angiography.
